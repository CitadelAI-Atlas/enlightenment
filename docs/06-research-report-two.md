## Findings

### 1. The Grof music protocol: primary source found

**Grade: verified, qualitative only.** Pass one could not locate the protocol;
pass two found it in the official Grof Transpersonal Training document
"Principles of Holotropic Breathwork." The primary text specifies a five-phase
music arc: opening or activating music, then trance-inducing music, then
breakthrough music (placed "about an hour and a half" into the session), then
heart music, then meditative music, with intensity decreasing through the
second half. Two important precisions:

- **There are no numeric phase proportions or timings in the primary text.**
  The sequence is described as a "statistical average" to be modified by the
  group's energy. Minute-by-minute schedules circulating online come from
  practitioner blogs, not from Grof.
- **Our five movements map one-to-one onto Grof's five phases** (Threshold to
  opening, Ascent to trance-inducing, Peak to breakthrough, Heart to heart,
  Return to meditative), and the one timing anchor the text offers (the
  breakthrough about ninety minutes into a session that typically runs two to
  three hours) is consistent with our peak placement.

**Consequence:** structural rule 3 ("rhythm front-loaded, melody back-loaded")
is upgraded from traditional practice to verified qualitative protocol. The
absence of numeric proportions in the primary source also validates our
normalized-contour approach: the tradition itself treats the arc as a shape,
not a schedule. The frozen v1 contour is unchanged.

### 2. Respiratory entrainment to music: H1's premise is now supported

**Grade: moderate at the group level; our specific slow envelope remains
untested.** Three primary studies:

- Bernardi, Porta and Sleight 2006 (Heart 92(4):445-452, DOI
  10.1136/hrt.2005.064600, n=24): breathing rate rises significantly with
  music tempo, entraining at sub-multiples of the beat (about one breath per
  four beats at slow tempi, one per eight at fast). Musicians entrain more.
  Respiration saturates at high tempo, so the scaling is not strictly
  proportional.
- Haas, Distenfeld and Axen 1986 (Journal of Applied Physiology
  61(3):1185-1191, n=20): musical rhythm reduces breath-period variability
  (coefficient of variation 23 percent at baseline, 15 percent listening, 10
  percent listening plus tapping). Tight entrainment appeared in 12 of 20
  subjects and not consistently within subjects; it was strongest under
  tapping, a motor task.
- Watanabe, Ooishi and Kashino 2015 (PLOS ONE 10(8):e0135589, n=52): the
  autonomic effect of fast tempo is **gated by respiratory rate**; fast tempo
  alone did not raise sympathetic tone unless breathing was also fast. The
  breath is the causal channel through which tempo reaches the autonomic
  system.

**Consequence:** H1's premise (listeners' breathing follows musical pacing) is
upgraded from "evidence not located" to group-level supported. The Watanabe
gating finding strengthens H1's logic: if respiration gates tempo's autonomic
effects upward, a slow breath-paced envelope plausibly gates them downward.
But the specific claim, entrainment to a slow amplitude envelope at 5.5 to 8
breaths per minute during passive listening, has never been tested. About
half of individuals may not entrain. H1 stays novel and is now better
motivated; expect group-level effects with individual variability.

### 3. Drumming and theta: weak as power induction, emerging as neural tracking

**Grade: mixed; relabeled accordingly.** The original claim rests on a single
unpublished dissertation (Maxfield 1990, n=12): drumming at roughly 4 to 4.5
beats per second was "the most inducting for theta gain," with effects only
when the pattern was sustained at least 13 to 15 minutes. A later attempt
(n=24, comparable pattern) found essentially no theta and decreased alpha,
explicitly failing to replicate (with measurement caveats and a low-tier
publisher). The frequently cited Gingras et al. 2014 study measured cortisol
and mood, no EEG at all. The strongest recent evidence points somewhere more
specific: a 2026 Scientific Reports study (DOI 10.1038/s41598-026-37700-x,
n=39) found **neural tracking at 4 Hz during 4-beat-per-second drumming**
correlated with self-reported altered experience (rho=0.39, p=.01),
frequency-specific against 1.5 and 9.5 Hz controls. Tracking the rhythm is
not the same mechanism as raising spontaneous theta power.

**Consequence:** the drumming layer's label changes from "traditional
precedent" to "mixed evidence; emerging support for frequency-specific neural
tracking." Two design choices turn out to align with the better-supported
findings: our rate sits at 3 to 4.5 beats per second, and our Ascent sustains
it well past the 13-to-15-minute threshold Maxfield reported.

### 4. Silence at peak intensity: the gap is now confirmed, and our trial is a first

**Grade: untested, definitively.** No controlled study anywhere places
near-silence at maximum intensity inside an ongoing receptive session. The
closest design, Bernardi 2006's two-minute pause randomly inserted between
tracks, produced **the lowest heart rate, blood pressure, and ventilation of
the entire protocol, below pre-music baseline**, which is striking adjacent
support for silence as an active ingredient. Stolterfoth and Pfeifer 2025
confirm the thinness of the field (5 of 89 screened studies). A first
adequately powered test should measure within-session arousal (heart rate and
variability, respiration, electrodermal activity) plus absorption, comparing
quiet-interval-present against absent. That is exactly our planned variable,
so the trial protocol will be written to that standard.

### 5. Long-form engagement and habituation: still not located

No claims on monotony, mind-wandering, dropout, or optimal variation density
in extended receptive listening survived verification. **This gap is real and
unanswered**, which means our own trial data (absorption ratings, per-movement
notes, completion rates from the player's public session reports) may be
among the first systematic evidence collected on it. The question stays open
rather than being padded with weak sources.

### Refuted in verification

One claim died (votes 1-2): an interpretation of Gingras et al. 2014 holding
that instruction context rather than the drumming itself drives reported
trance. The adversarial panel found the inference overreached the study's
design. Recorded here because killed claims are part of the method.
